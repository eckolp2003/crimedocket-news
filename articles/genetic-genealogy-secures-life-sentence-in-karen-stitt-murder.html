Genetic Genealogy Secures Life Sentence in Karen Stitt Murder

A direct DNA match derived from blood preserved on a cinderblock wall led a Santa Clara County judge to sentence Gary Gene Ramirez to 25 years to life for a 1982 homicide.

Crime Docket March 24, 2026 613 words · 3 min read

Biological evidence preserved on a victim’s leather jacket and an adjacent cinderblock wall for more than four decades culminated in a 25-years-to-life sentence on Monday. In the Santa Clara County Superior Court, a judge sentenced 78-year-old Gary Gene Ramirez for the 1982 Karen Stitt murder, concluding a prosecution built entirely on investigative genetic genealogy (IGG). The sentencing, reported by KTVU on May 12, 2025, closes a cold case homicide that long frustrated California law enforcement.

Exclusionary DNA and the Genetic Pivot

The prosecution’s case against Ramirez relied on blood and bodily fluids left at the Sunnyvale, California crime scene on the morning of September 3, 1982. The 15-year-old victim suffered 59 stab wounds, but the perpetrator left behind a substantial biological profile during the commission of the homicide.

Initially, investigators focused on the victim’s teenage boyfriend, who was the last person to see her alive at a bus stop near El Camino Real and Wolfe Road. However, according to case records, advancements in Short Tandem Repeat (STR) analysis—a forensic method used to compare specific loci on DNA from two or more samples—completely cleared the boyfriend in 2000.

With the primary suspect excluded, the biological evidence remained in police custody until 2019. At that time, Sunnyvale Department of Public Safety Detective Matt Hutchison initiated an IGG review of the suspect’s DNA profile. IGG combines traditional DNA phenotyping with genealogical research to identify suspects through their genetic relatives.

Kinship Analysis to Direct Match

Forensic genealogists uploaded the perpetrator’s genetic profile to proprietary databases, successfully tracing the DNA to a family tree rooted in Fresno, California. The data pointed to four brothers, including Gary Gene Ramirez.

To narrow the suspect pool, investigators utilized kinship analysis. In April 2022, law enforcement secured a DNA sample from one of Ramirez’s biological children. Laboratory analysis confirmed a high-probability parent-child relationship between the child and the unknown contributor of the 1982 crime scene DNA.

Armed with this statistical probability, Santa Clara County authorities obtained a search warrant for Ramirez, who was residing in Makawao, Hawaii. Investigators executed the warrant in August 2022, collecting a direct buccal swab (a swab of the inner cheek used to collect cellular material) from Ramirez. The California Department of Justice laboratory confirmed that Ramirez’s direct sample was a definitive match to the blood recovered from the victim’s leather jacket and the cinderblock wall.

Conviction in Santa Clara County Superior Court

Following a multi-agency extradition process from Maui to California, prosecutors charged Ramirez with first-degree murder. Facing insurmountable forensic evidence, Ramirez entered a plea of no contest on February 24, 2025.

According to KTVU reporters monitoring the proceedings, the Santa Clara County Superior Court finalized the case on May 12, 2025, handing down the mandatory sentence of 25 years to life in state prison. The resolution of the Karen Stitt murder highlights the increasing reliance on biological preservation and familial tracing to resolve dormant homicides.

The Evidence

  • Crime Scene Serology: Blood and bodily fluids belonging to the perpetrator were preserved on the victim’s leather jacket and a nearby cinderblock wall in Sunnyvale, California.
  • Exclusionary Testing: Early STR DNA analysis conducted in 2000 conclusively eliminated the initial primary suspect (the victim’s boyfriend) as the source of the biological material.
  • Genealogical Profile: A 2019 IGG analysis traced the suspect DNA to a specific familial line originating in Fresno, isolating four brothers.
  • Kinship DNA: A DNA sample collected from Ramirez’s child in April 2022 provided a high-probability paternal match to the crime scene profile.
  • Direct DNA Match: A court-ordered buccal swab taken from Ramirez in August 2022 matched the crime scene blood evidence directly, securing the arrest and subsequent conviction.
