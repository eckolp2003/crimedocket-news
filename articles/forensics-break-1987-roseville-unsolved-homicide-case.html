Forensics Break 1987 Roseville Unsolved Homicide Case

Advancements in forensic technology have led the Roseville Police Department to identify a deceased person of interest in a 38-year-old cold case, though evidence suggests an accomplice remains at large.

Crime Docket March 26, 2026 959 words · 4 min read

Advancements in modern forensic technology have broken a 38-year investigative stalemate in the 1987 Roseville unsolved homicide of 22-year-old Sue Capistrant. On July 18, 2025, the Roseville Police Department formally named Robert Osborne—a local man who died in 1996—as a person of interest, citing new associative data linking him to the victim during the 24-hour window surrounding her death.

While identifying Osborne represents the most significant breakthrough in the case’s history, crime scene parameters and spatial evidence have led detectives to conclude that Osborne did not act alone. Authorities are now utilizing public appeals to bridge a critical gap in the data: establishing a concrete historical or social nexus between the victim, the identified person of interest, and a potential living accomplice.

Forensic Advancements Point to Robert Osborne

For nearly four decades, the Capistrant case file remained active, with investigators processing over 100 witness interviews and evaluating more than a dozen potential suspects. However, physical evidence recovered from the 1987 crime scene had exhausted standard testing methodologies available in the late 20th century.

In July 2025, Roseville Police Detective Brady Martin and Deputy Chief Joe Adams announced that utilizing recent advancements in forensic technology yielded a definitive link to Robert Osborne, according to reports from CBS News Minnesota/WCCO. Osborne, born in 1963, was a similar age to Capistrant at the time of the homicide. He resided in Roseville but died in 1996, categorizing his potential future clearance in this case as an “exceptional clearance” (a case resolution where law enforcement has gathered enough evidence to arrest and charge a suspect, but cannot do so because the suspect is deceased).

Law enforcement has deliberately withheld the specific nature of the forensic linkage—whether it is DNA profiling, latent print recovery via advanced chemical processing, or trace evidence—to maintain the integrity of the ongoing investigation. However, investigators confirmed that the forensic data places Capistrant in direct contact with Osborne either on April 7 or in the early hours of April 8, 1987.

Timeline Analysis: The Night of April 7-8, 1987

To understand the context of the forensic evidence, analysts must look at the spatial and chronological data established by law enforcement prior to the discovery of the body.

  • Evening of April 7: Capistrant was last seen in a social setting with her brother and a friend at Patrick’s Lounge, a venue located in St. Paul, Minnesota.
  • Early Hours of April 8: Following the outing, Capistrant and her friend returned to Capistrant’s parents’ residence, located on the 1300 block of Garden Avenue in Roseville.
  • The Midnight Call: At approximately midnight, Capistrant received a telephone call from an unknown individual. Investigative records indicate this conversation was brief.
  • 1:00 a.m. Departure: Following the phone call, Capistrant and her friend departed the Garden Avenue residence. According to FOX 9 Minneapolis-St. Paul, the friend was quickly cleared by law enforcement through alibi verification and physical evidence, and is not considered a suspect.
  • 7:30 a.m. Discovery: At 7:30 a.m. on April 8, an 11-year-old girl discovered Capistrant’s body behind a grocery store situated at the intersection of Dale Street and County Road B in Roseville.

The critical investigative window spans roughly six and a half hours—between the 1:00 a.m. departure and the 7:30 a.m. discovery. The midnight phone call represents a key behavioral data point, suggesting Capistrant’s movement from the residence was premeditated and coordinated with an external party.

The Missing Link and Accomplice Theory

Despite the forensic link to Osborne, a distinct data vacuum remains: analysts have been unable to establish a historical social connection between Osborne and Capistrant prior to the murder.

Furthermore, the physical dynamics of the crime scene strongly suggest multiple actors. The Roseville Police Department has stated publicly that they believe more than one person was involved in the homicide. In forensic pathology and crime scene reconstruction, a multi-suspect hypothesis is often supported by specific data points, such as:

  1. Transport and Disposal Mechanics: The physical effort required to move or conceal a body often leaves bilateral trace evidence or requires leverage indicating two distinct individuals.
  2. Staging: Contradictory physical evidence at the scene (e.g., footprints indicating different stride lengths or shoe sizes).
  3. Digital/Telephonic Timelines: Accomplices are frequently identified through split timelines, where one suspect is managing logistics (like the midnight phone call) while another executes the primary offense.

The necessity for public crowdsourcing of historical data is critical here. While open-source intelligence efforts—such as the 2020 Facebook group “Who Killed Susan Capistrant” initiated by high school acquaintance Corey Prescott—have generated localized interest, law enforcement requires specific witness testimony. Investigators are urging anyone with historical knowledge of Robert Osborne, or anyone who observed him in proximity to Capistrant or the Dale Street/County Road B intersection in 1987, to provide that data to the Roseville Police Department.

The Evidence

  • Victim Demographics: Sue Capistrant, 22-year-old female.
  • Crime Scene Location: Behind a grocery store at the intersection of Dale Street and County Road B, Roseville, Minnesota. Body discovered at 7:30 a.m. on April 8, 1987.
  • Timeline Anomaly: A midnight phone call from an unknown individual precipitated the victim’s departure from a safe location at 1:00 a.m.
  • Forensic Identification: Advanced forensic technology linked Robert Osborne (1963–1996) to the victim during the specific timeframe of the offense.
  • Multi-Actor Hypothesis: Based on crime scene data, investigators assess with high confidence that Osborne did not act alone, and are actively seeking an accomplice who may still be living.

The integration of modern forensic science with a static 1987 cold case has provided a critical breakthrough, but the investigation relies on historical social network data to achieve a final resolution. Crime Docket will continue to monitor the Roseville Police Department’s data releases regarding the secondary suspect.
