DNA Solves 1994 Infant Cold Case: Monterey County Mother Sentenced

Advanced forensic testing funded by a DOJ grant concludes a 30-year investigation into the abandonment of a newborn known as 'Baby Garin.'

Crime Docket March 24, 2026 567 words · 2 min read

Advanced DNA forensic testing funded by a U.S. Department of Justice grant has resulted in a 13-year, four-month state prison sentence in the 1994 manslaughter of a newborn infant in Monterey County, California.

On February 18, 2026, Monterey County Superior Court Judge Pamela L. Butler sentenced 61-year-old Pamela Ferreyra to state prison. The conviction ends a 30-year investigation driven by the Monterey County District Attorney’s Office Cold Case Task Force, which utilized modern genetic analysis to link Ferreyra to the recovered remains.

Forensic Identification of “Baby Garin”

The investigation originated on December 3, 1994, when an individual collecting aluminum cans discovered the partial remains of a 2-to-3-day-old infant inside a red paper grocery bag off Garin Road in Prunedale, California. According to the Monterey County District Attorney’s Office, the unidentified victim was locally designated “Baby John Doe” or “Baby Garin.”

Initial post-mortem medical examinations determined the infant had been born alive outside of a clinical hospital setting. The autopsy report indicated the child had not been fed for approximately 24 hours prior to his death. While the precise physiological cause of death remained indeterminate at the time, investigators noted the absence of any corresponding missing persons reports.

The case remained dormant until July 2020, when Monterey County District Attorney Jeannine M. Pacioni established a dedicated Cold Case Task Force. Utilizing a 2021 U.S. Department of Justice grant allocated specifically for prosecuting unsolved cases, investigators submitted biological samples from the infant’s remains for advanced DNA analysis. By 2024, the forensic testing successfully isolated Pamela Ferreyra, a resident of Watsonville, California, as the biological mother.

Custodial Admissions and Plea Agreement

Detectives from the Monterey County Sheriff’s Office apprehended Ferreyra at her Watsonville residence on October 17, 2024. During subsequent custodial interviews, Ferreyra provided statements corroborating the forensic findings. She admitted to concealing her pregnancy from her immediate family, delivering the infant alive at her residence, and subsequently transporting him in her vehicle to the remote Garin Road location where he was abandoned.

On December 3, 2025—exactly 31 years after the discovery of the infant’s remains—Ferreyra entered a guilty plea to one count of voluntary manslaughter and one count of felony child abuse. As part of the plea agreement, she admitted to a special legal allegation that she inflicted great bodily injury on the infant during the commission of the offense.

Sentencing Under California Law

The charges levied against Ferreyra are classified as serious and violent felonies, thereby constituting strikes under California’s Three Strikes Law (a sentencing statute that mandates enhanced penalties for repeat offenders convicted of serious or violent felonies). The 13-year and four-month sentence handed down by Judge Butler represents the statutory outcome of the plea terms.

According to the District Attorney’s Office, Ferreyra’s sentencing marks the tenth successful homicide conviction secured by the Monterey County DA’s Cold Case Task Force since its formation.

The Evidence

  • Victim: Unidentified male infant (“Baby Garin”), discovered December 3, 1994, in Prunedale, California.
  • Forensic Findings: Autopsy established the infant was born alive outside a hospital and unfed for 24 hours prior to death.
  • DNA Link: 2024 genetic analysis funded by a DOJ grant confirmed Pamela Ferreyra as the biological mother.
  • Defendant Admissions: Ferreyra confessed to the live home birth and subsequent abandonment of the infant.
  • Court Action: Pled guilty to voluntary manslaughter and felony child abuse, resulting in a 13-year, four-month prison sentence.
