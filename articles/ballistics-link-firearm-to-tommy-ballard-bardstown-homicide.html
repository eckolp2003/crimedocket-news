Ballistics Link Firearm to Tommy Ballard Bardstown Homicide

Prosecutors state a recovered rifle matches four of five forensic markers linking it to the single bullet that killed the Kentucky father in 2016.

Crime Docket March 26, 2026 497 words · 2 min read

Ballistics testing has aligned four out of five forensic criteria linking a recovered rifle to the single projectile that killed 54-year-old Thomas “Tommy” Ballard in 2016. As the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) maintains its active homicide classification for the Bardstown, Kentucky case, a recently augmented $50,000 reward pool is driving renewed public focus on the evidence, according to recent statements from the Ballard family published by WHAS11.

Ballistic Links to the Suspected Weapon

During an October 5, 2023, bond hearing in Nelson Circuit Court for Brooks Houck—the convicted ex-boyfriend of Ballard’s missing daughter, Crystal Rogers—Special Prosecutor Shane Young entered critical forensic data into the public record. Young announced that investigators had taken custody of the rifle believed to be the murder weapon used in the Ballard homicide.

According to the prosecution’s court statements, ballistics analysis of the rifle yielded a high-probability match, satisfying four out of five necessary criteria to definitively link the firearm to the bullet recovered from Ballard’s body. Furthermore, investigators allege the weapon was purchased from Nick Houck, Brooks Houck’s brother and a former Bardstown Police Officer, who utilized a pseudonym during the transaction.

Crime Scene Reconstruction

Crime scene investigators and the FBI established the parameters of the homicide shortly after the November 19, 2016, incident. Ballard was shot once in the chest from behind while preparing for a hunting trip on private property adjacent to the Bluegrass Parkway, near the 1400 block of Ed Brent Road.

Law enforcement rapidly eliminated two initial theories: suicide and accidental discharge. Forensic technicians confirmed Ballard’s personal firearm had not been discharged, ruling out a self-inflicted wound. Additionally, investigators explicitly cleared Ballard’s 12-year-old grandson, who was present at the scene, of any involvement. The FBI officially designated the fatal shooting as an intentional homicide.

Establishing an Investigative Motive

Investigative theories surrounding the assassination center on Ballard’s proximity to uncovering physical evidence regarding his daughter’s 2015 disappearance. Prior to his death, Ballard organized exhaustive, independent searches of Nelson County’s wooded areas and waterways.

Statements provided to WHAS11 by Ballard’s relatives indicate a working theory shared by the family and law enforcement: Ballard was targeted because “he was getting too close” to physical evidence or burial sites connected to the Rogers case. Months before the shooting, Ballard formally reported to his wife that he was being subjected to vehicular surveillance.

The Evidence

  • Cause of Death: Single gunshot wound to the chest, fired from behind.
  • Weapon Recovery: Investigators seized a rifle matching four of five ballistics criteria to the fatal bullet.
  • Chain of Custody: The suspected murder weapon was allegedly sold under a false name by former police officer Nick Houck.
  • Crime Scene Status: Accidental discharge and suicide were forensically ruled out by analyzing the victim’s undischarged firearm.

The FBI’s Louisville Field Office continues to solicit public assistance in the active investigation. Individuals with actionable intelligence are directed to contact the FBI tip line at 1-800-CALL-FBI (1-800-225-5324) or submit documentation through tips.fbi.gov.
