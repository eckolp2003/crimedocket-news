Forensic Review Advances Nairn Banker Murder Cold Case Inquiry

Police Scotland applies modern DNA techniques and completes a structural review of 10,000 documents in the 2004 unsolved shooting of Alistair Wilson.

Crime Docket March 24, 2026 933 words · 4 min read

A comprehensive forensic and documentary review encompassing 10,000 documents, 6,000 witness statements, and 14,000 individual police actions marks a structural pivot in the 2004 Nairn Banker Murder cold case. Police Scotland and the Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service (COPFS) recently confirmed to the BBC that a “significant amount of work” has been completed in the reinvestigation into the fatal shooting of 30-year-old Alistair Wilson.

The reinvestigation, mandated in September 2024 by Scotland’s Lord Advocate, Dorothy Bain KC, operates under the designation Operation Sabine. Led by Chief Superintendent Suzanne Chow, a dedicated team of up to 37 officers and staff is applying modern forensic analysis to 20-year-old evidence, aiming to resolve one of the Scottish Highlands’ most persistent investigative anomalies.

Operation Sabine: Data Review and Forensic DNA Analysis

The foundation of Operation Sabine relies on systematically dismantling and re-evaluating the original investigation parameters. By auditing the 14,000 individual police actions conducted since November 2004, analysts are cross-referencing legacy suspect statements against contemporary database capabilities.

A primary focus of the renewed inquiry lies in forensic advancements. Investigators are exploring “new opportunities” utilizing modern DNA analysis technologies that were unavailable at the time of the offense. This includes the potential extraction of touch DNA (microscopic epithelial cells left behind via skin contact) from retained physical evidence, such as ballistics materials and items recovered near the Crescent Road crime scene in Nairn.

Ballistics Profile: The Haenel Suhl Model 1 Pistol

The central piece of physical evidence in the Nairn Banker Murder is the firearm, which was recovered days after the shooting by municipal council workers clearing a drain in the town.

Forensic ballistics experts identified the weapon as a 1920s/1930s-era 0.25 calibre Haenel Suhl Model 1 Schmeisser’s patent pocket pistol. However, the ammunition recovered was not contemporary to the antique firearm. Toolmark and manufacturing analysis traced the bullets to Sellier and Bellot, a manufacturer based in the Czech Republic, produced between 1983 and 1993.

This specific chronological discrepancy between the weapon and the ammunition points to a highly specific procurement channel, ruling out a simple opportunistic purchase and suggesting a degree of premeditation in sourcing functioning ammunition for a vintage pocket pistol.

Crime Scene Trace: The Blue Envelope

The tactical execution of the murder presents distinct behavioral signatures. At approximately 7:00 PM on November 28, 2004, the offender rang the doorbell of the Wilson family residence. The suspect—described as a stocky male wearing a dark jacket and baseball cap—specifically asked for Alistair by name when Wilson’s wife answered the door.

Upon Wilson coming to the door, the suspect presented a blue envelope with the name “Paul” written on the exterior. Wilson took the envelope inside, discovered it was empty, and returned to the threshold to confront the individual. He was subsequently shot three times at point-blank range.

Crucially, the offender retrieved the blue envelope before fleeing the scene. By taking the envelope, the assailant actively denied forensic investigators access to primary trace evidence, including potential handwriting exemplars, latent fingerprints, and cellular material. The use of an empty envelope suggests it was deployed as a prop—a tactical distraction designed to confuse the victim and dictate the positioning for the firearm discharge.

Suspect Motive Matrix

Over two decades, police inquiries—which included interviews with over 11,000 individuals and international investigative travel to Nova Scotia, Canada—have centered on two primary hypotheses regarding motive.

In 2022, Police Scotland publicly focused on a localized grievance: a planning dispute. Days prior to his murder, Wilson submitted a formal objection to a retrospective planning application (a request for legal permission for construction that has already occurred or is underway) concerning a large decking area at the Havelock Hotel, located directly opposite the Wilson residence.

Alternatively, independent crime analysts and former detectives have evaluated the highly targeted nature of the hit, suggesting the use of a specialized firearm and the specific targeting of Wilson points toward his professional capacity as a business manager for the Bank of Scotland, where he managed corporate loans and debts.

Institutional Oversight and Protocol Shifts

The initiation of Operation Sabine follows structural friction between the Wilson family and law enforcement. In 2023, the family lodged a formal complaint with the Police Investigation and Review Commissioner (PIRC), the independent body responsible for investigating incidents involving the police in Scotland.

According to the family’s statements, a breakdown in trust occurred after police leadership aborted a planned operation to arrest a key suspect in May 2023. This operational dispute escalated to the highest levels of Scottish government, culminating in a meeting between First Minister John Swinney, Wilson’s widow Veronica, and their eldest son Andrew in early 2025. The ensuing political and public scrutiny directly precipitated the Lord Advocate’s mandate for the current, ground-up reinvestigation.

The Evidence

  • The Weapon: A 1920s/1930s-era 0.25 calibre Haenel Suhl Model 1 Schmeisser’s patent pocket pistol, recovered from a municipal drain in Nairn.
  • The Ammunition: Czech-manufactured Sellier and Bellot 0.25 calibre rounds, produced between 1983 and 1993.
  • The Suspect: Described by an eyewitness as a stocky male wearing a baseball cap and dark jacket.
  • Tactical Element: An empty blue envelope marked “Paul” was handed to the victim and subsequently retrieved by the killer post-shooting, removing key trace evidence from the scene.
  • Investigative Scope: Operation Sabine encompasses a review of 10,000 documents, 6,000 witness statements, and 14,000 individual police actions recorded since 2004.

As Operation Sabine proceeds under the supervision of the COPFS, the integration of 2025 forensic capabilities with the 2004 physical evidence remains the most viable pathway to identifying the perpetrator in this landmark Scottish cold case.
