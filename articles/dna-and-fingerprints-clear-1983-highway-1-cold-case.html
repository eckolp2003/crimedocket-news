DNA and Fingerprints Clear 1983 Highway 1 Cold Case

Blood evidence analyzed by genetic genealogists and a latent print on a soda can identified two deceased Texas men as perpetrators in the 1983 murder of Dorothy Tate.

Crime Docket March 24, 2026 944 words · 4 min read

Blood DNA extracted from a decades-old crime scene and a latent fingerprint lifted from a discarded soda can have provided the evidentiary data required to close the 1983 murder investigation of Dorothy “Toby” Tate. On January 29, 2026, the San Luis Obispo (SLO) County Sheriff’s Department announced the formal closure of the case, utilizing modern identity inference pipelines and cross-agency forensic collaboration to name two suspects over four decades after the homicide.

The investigation, officially designated as exceptionally cleared, highlights the increasing efficacy of Forensic Genetic Genealogy (FGG) in resolving stagnant cases where traditional law enforcement databases yield no matches.

The Forensic Genealogy Pipeline

On November 15, 1983, investigators discovered Tate’s body inside a 1970s Chevrolet van parked at a turnout on Highway 1, approximately four miles north of Hearst Castle in California. The cause of death was determined to be a fatal gunshot wound. Initial evidence collection at the crime scene secured a vital biological sample: blood evidence that did not belong to the victim.

At the time of the murder, DNA profiling was not yet an established forensic tool. When the blood evidence was later processed and uploaded to the Combined DNA Index System (CODIS), it failed to generate a matching profile. The case remained cold, with the only alternative lead being a stolen camera traced to a pawn shop years later—a dead end due to the item passing through multiple undocumented owners.

The trajectory of the investigation shifted when San Luis Obispo County Cold Case/Unsolved Homicide Detective Clint Cole reopened the file. Recognizing the potential of advanced molecular biology, the SLO Sheriff’s Crime Lab, under the direction of forensic specialist Shelby Liddell, submitted the preserved blood evidence for advanced testing.

The laboratory partnered with two prominent private forensics firms: Othram Labs and Parabon Nanolabs. Unlike traditional STR (Short Tandem Repeat) testing used in standard law enforcement databases, these facilities utilize Forensic-Grade Genome Sequencing to analyze hundreds of thousands of Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs). This methodology allows geneticists to determine distant familial relationships.

Head Genealogist CeCe Moore assisted in analyzing the genealogical data. By building extensive family trees based on the genetic markers found in the crime scene blood, investigators narrowed the suspect pool to a specific lineage, eventually identifying Steven Richard Hardy of Texas as the source of the biological material. According to the SLO Sheriff’s Department, investigators verified the identity by comparing the crime scene DNA to a sample obtained from a close living relative of Hardy.

This identification marks the 74th publicly announced California cold case successfully resolved utilizing Othram’s identity inference pipeline.

Latent Print Recovery and Secondary Identification

While DNA analysis isolated the primary contributor of the blood evidence, investigators relied on traditional friction ridge analysis to identify a second suspect present at the scene.

During the initial 1983 scene processing, crime scene technicians recovered a discarded soda can. Forensic personnel successfully lifted a latent fingerprint from the aluminum surface. Latent prints are formed by the natural oils and sweat on human skin, which are transferred to smooth surfaces. When preserved properly, these prints can remain viable for comparison indefinitely.

For decades, the print remained unmatched in the Automated Fingerprint Identification System (AFIS). However, re-evaluating the physical evidence in tandem with the new DNA leads allowed investigators to identify a second Texas man, Charlie Sneed, as the source of the fingerprint. The cross-agency effort to confirm the print involved Dominick Rostenberg from the Phoenix Police Department, operating in collaboration with the SLO Sheriff’s Crime Lab.

Exceptionally Cleared Status

Despite the definitive forensic links connecting both Hardy and Sneed to the 1983 crime scene, neither man will face judicial prosecution. Both suspects are deceased.

Following the forensic identifications, the San Luis Obispo County Sheriff’s Department, under the administration of Sheriff Ian Parkinson, presented the comprehensive case file to the District Attorney’s Office. Prosecutors reviewed the DNA match, the latent print analysis, and the corroborating investigative data. The District Attorney concluded that the evidentiary threshold for probable cause had been met—meaning that, had the suspects been alive, the state possessed sufficient evidence to file formal homicide charges and secure arrest warrants.

Because the suspects are deceased, the SLO County Sheriff’s Department has formally closed the investigation under the designation of “exceptionally cleared.” In law enforcement terminology, an exceptional clearance occurs when an agency has established the identity of the offender and gathered enough evidence to support an arrest, charge, and prosecution, but is prevented from initiating legal proceedings by circumstances outside the control of law enforcement—most commonly, the death of the perpetrator.

The Evidence

  • Victim: Dorothy “Toby” Tate, 41, of Estes Park, Colorado.
  • Crime Scene: A 1970s Chevrolet van parked on a Highway 1 turnout, four miles north of Hearst Castle, California.
  • Date of Discovery: November 15, 1983.
  • Cause of Death: Gunshot wound.
  • Biological Evidence: Blood recovered from the crime scene, previously unmatched in CODIS, underwent Forensic-Grade Genome Sequencing via Othram Labs and Parabon Nanolabs.
  • Genealogical Identification: DNA analysis and familial comparison identified Steven Richard Hardy (Texas) as the contributor of the blood.
  • Physical Evidence: A latent fingerprint lifted from a soda can at the crime scene in 1983 was definitively matched to a second suspect, Charlie Sneed (Texas).
  • Resolution: Both suspects are deceased. The District Attorney confirmed probable cause existed for prosecution, resulting in an “exceptionally cleared” case status.

The resolution of the Dorothy Tate homicide underscores the critical importance of long-term evidence preservation. By maintaining the integrity of biological samples and latent prints gathered in 1983, forensic scientists ensured that the data remained viable for decades, waiting for analytical technology to evolve sufficiently to interpret it.
