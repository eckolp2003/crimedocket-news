Hair Match & Confession Close 1962 Carol Ann Dougherty Cold Case

Microscopic hair analysis and a familial confession have conclusively identified a Louisiana inmate as the perpetrator in a 63-year-old Bucks County homicide.

Crime Docket March 25, 2026 1022 words · 4 min read

Microscopic hair comparison, combined with a corroborated informant confession, has officially resolved the 1962 Carol Ann Dougherty cold case. On October 29, 2025, the Bucks County District Attorney’s Office, acting on the findings of a grand jury, formally identified William Schrader as the perpetrator in the 63-year-old homicide. The resolution relies on decades of forensic preservation and a 2024 witness interview that corroborated physical evidence collected from the Bristol Borough crime scene.

Forensic Elimination and Physical Evidence

The evidentiary foundation of this case rests heavily on trace evidence collected during the initial 1962 investigation. When the nine-year-old victim’s body was discovered, investigators secured physical evidence from the scene, including pubic hairs found clutched in the victim’s hand and deposited on her thighs.

Due to the technological limitations of the era, definitive DNA profiling was unavailable. However, evidence technicians properly stored the biological samples, preserving them for future analysis. In 1993, investigators utilized microscopic hair comparison—a forensic technique analyzing the morphological characteristics of hair shafts—to test the suspect sample against elimination samples (biological material collected from known individuals to exclude them from the suspect pool).

According to records released by the Bucks County District Attorney’s Office, detectives collected and tested hair samples from 141 different men over the decades. William Schrader, who was 22 years old and lived less than two blocks from the crime scene at the time of the murder, was the only individual whose sample could not be excluded. Forensic analysts concluded that Schrader’s sample exhibited “significant similarities” to the trace evidence found on the victim.

Additional physical evidence at the scene included discarded Lucky Strike cigarettes. Investigators documented that Lucky Strike was the specific brand Schrader smoked during that time period.

Alibi Discrepancies and Behavioral Indicators

Schrader was considered a person of interest shortly after the homicide. Early investigative reports indicate that his alibi for October 22, 1962, quickly collapsed under scrutiny.

Schrader initially told police he was at work during the time of the murder. However, investigators subpoenaed his employment records and discovered his timecards proved he was not at his workplace when the crime occurred. Furthermore, police administered a polygraph examination, which Schrader failed, indicating deception regarding his involvement in the homicide. Despite these strong circumstantial indicators, prosecutors lacked the definitive forensic link required to secure a conviction in 1962.

The 2024 Informant Confession

The definitive breakthrough that prompted the grand jury finding occurred in late 2024. In November, detectives from the Bucks County District Attorney’s Office and the Pennsylvania State Police interviewed Robert Leblanc, Schrader’s stepson.

According to statements provided to law enforcement, Leblanc disclosed that Schrader had explicitly confessed to the crime on two separate occasions prior to his death. Leblanc informed detectives that Schrader admitted to luring a young girl inside a Catholic church in Pennsylvania before relocating to the American South. Crucially, Schrader stated he “had to kill the girl in Bristol to keep her from talking” following a sexual assault. This confession accurately matched the specific location and victimology of the Dougherty homicide, information that tightly corroborated the 1993 forensic findings.

Crime Scene Pathology and Victimology

The physical parameters of the crime scene and the victim’s pathology align with an opportunistic, predatory attack. On October 22, 1962, Carol Ann Dougherty stopped at St. Mark’s Roman Catholic Church in Bristol Borough, Pennsylvania, to pray after visiting the Bristol Free Library.

When she failed to return home, her father initiated a search, locating her blue-and-white Londoner bicycle outside the church. Inside, investigators discovered the victim’s body concealed beneath a stained-glass window in the church’s choir loft.

Forensic pathologists determined the cause of death was ligature strangulation (asphyxiation caused by a cord or similar object used to tie or bind the neck). The medical examiner also confirmed evidence of sexual assault, which directly matched the details of the confession later provided by Leblanc.

Offender Custodial History

William Schrader died in 2002 at the age of 62 while incarcerated in a Louisiana state prison, decades before formal charges could be filed in Pennsylvania.

His custodial history following the Bristol Borough murder demonstrates an escalating pattern of violent offenses. Bucks County District Attorney Jennifer Schorn characterized Schrader as an “absolute predator,” noting that authorities documented a history of sexual abuse involving nearly every female child Schrader lived with or had access to throughout his life.

His final incarceration in Louisiana stemmed from convictions for manslaughter and arson. In 1985, Schrader intentionally set his own residence on fire, an act that resulted in the death of a 12-year-old family member.

The Evidence

  • Trace Evidence Match: Microscopic hair analysis conducted in 1993 failed to exclude Schrader, showing significant morphological similarities to pubic hair found in the victim’s hand and on her thighs. Schrader was the only non-excluded subject out of 141 tested individuals.
  • Corroborated Confession: In November 2024, stepson Robert Leblanc informed Pennsylvania State Police and Bucks County detectives that Schrader confessed twice to raping and murdering a little girl in a Bristol church to prevent her from identifying him.
  • Alibi Failure: Schrader’s initial 1962 claim that he was at work during the murder was disproven by his official timecards.
  • Physical Scene Evidence: Discarded Lucky Strike cigarettes were found at the crime scene, matching Schrader’s specific brand preference at the time.
  • Proximity: At the time of the murder, Schrader resided on Lincoln Avenue, approximately one-and-a-half blocks from St. Mark’s Roman Catholic Church.

Official Case Closure

Because the perpetrator is deceased, the criminal justice system cannot pursue a standard trial. Instead, the Bucks County District Attorney’s Office utilized an investigating grand jury to review the totality of the evidence, effectively functioning as a formal fact-finding mechanism to close the case.

On October 29, 2025, DA Schorn officially announced the case closed. The victim’s younger sister, Kay Talanca, who was three years old when the crime occurred, noted during the press conference that the formal identification provided definitive answers after six decades of investigative pursuit. The case stands as a testament to the value of long-term evidence preservation and inter-agency cooperation between local detectives and the Pennsylvania State Police.
