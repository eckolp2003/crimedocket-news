DNA Match Solves 33-Year-Old Hillsboro Cold Case Murder

Advanced DNA extraction from swabs taken from the victim's collar definitively linked deceased serial killer Cesar Barone to a 1992 strangulation.

Crime Docket March 24, 2026 505 words · 2 min read

Swabs taken from the neckline of a preserved dress have yielded a definitive DNA profile, allowing investigators to close the 33-year-old Hillsboro cold case murder of Elizabeth Wasson. According to a February 2026 announcement by the Washington County Cold Case Unit, the genetic material belongs to deceased serial killer Cesar Barone. This empirical match resolves a 1992 investigation that lacked the physical evidence necessary for prosecution prior to the advent of modern DNA extraction techniques.

Advanced Extraction Yields Cesar Barone DNA Match

When 82-year-old Elizabeth Wasson was found strangled in her Hillsboro, Oregon home on September 23, 1992, DNA forensic science was still in its infancy. However, crime scene technicians properly preserved the victim’s clothing, maintaining the integrity of microscopic biological evidence for future analysis.

Three years ago, the Washington County Cold Case Unit, led by Hillsboro Police Detective Megan Townsend and Chief Deputy District Attorney Allison Brown, officially reopened the file. Forensic technicians utilized targeted swabbing around the collar of the dress Wasson was wearing at the time of her strangulation, aiming to capture epithelial cells (skin cells shed through friction) deposited by the attacker. Using 2026 DNA extraction and testing protocols, the laboratory successfully isolated a viable DNA profile. This data was subsequently verified, providing the definitive Cesar Barone DNA match required to officially close the case.

Modus Operandi and Prior Suspicions

Geographical profiling and an analysis of the perpetrator’s Modus Operandi (MO—a specific pattern of criminal behavior) initially pointed investigators toward Barone in the early 1990s. As reported by KCTV, Barone frequently targeted vulnerable or elderly women in the area, utilizing home invasions that culminated in sexual assault and manual strangulation. Following Wasson’s murder, three other elderly women in the area were murdered in a similar fashion, establishing a clear serial pattern.

Former detective Murray Ralph previously interviewed Barone in a prison infirmary regarding the unsolved murders, but Barone refused to confess. Investigators could not advance the case without biological or trace evidence linking him to the scene. Barone was arrested in 1995 for the murder of Martha Bryant, subsequently convicted of four additional murders, and died in prison in 2009. The recent forensic confirmation transforms three decades of circumstantial police suspicion into definitive proof.

The Evidence

  • The Crime: 82-year-old Elizabeth Wasson was found violently strangled in her Hillsboro residence on September 23, 1992.
  • The Biological Material: A definitive DNA profile was extracted in 2026 from swabs taken around the neck of the victim’s properly preserved dress.
  • The Match: Forensic laboratory results matched the extracted genetic profile to serial killer Cesar Barone.
  • Case Status: Although Barone died in prison in 2009 and cannot face prosecution, the Washington County Cold Case Unit has officially closed the investigation.

This resolution underscores the critical importance of long-term evidence retention policies, demonstrating how meticulous collection protocols from decades past enable modern forensic closures. The Washington County Cold Case Unit stated they will continue to apply these modern DNA testing methodologies to actively investigate remaining unsolved homicides within their jurisdiction.
