Blunt Force Trauma Misclassified at Compromised Ohio Scene

The 2017 homicide investigation of Amy Jo Nelson remains stalled after first responders failed to secure the scene, allowing chemical destruction of potential biological evidence.

Crime Docket March 26, 2026 839 words · 4 min read

The documented use of chemical cleaning agents at an unsecured death scene severely compromised the September 2017 homicide investigation of 39-year-old Amy Jo Nelson before her body was formally removed from the premises. First responders from the New Straitsville Police Department initially categorized the fatality as a suspected drug overdose, despite visible physiological signs of physical trauma. This initial misclassification led to a cascade of protocol deviations that continue to hinder the Perry County cold case today.

According to investigative reporting by NBC4 WCMH-TV, Nelson was discovered deceased on September 18, 2017, inside an apartment on Main Street in the village of New Straitsville, Ohio. While the initial police assessment concluded the death was overdose-related, the official autopsy mandate yielded contradictory findings, prompting the Ohio Bureau of Criminal Investigation (BCI) to eventually assist in the stalled inquiry.

Medico-Legal Death Investigation Protocol Deviations

Standard forensic protocol dictates that any unattended death must be treated as a homicide until post-mortem examination and scene processing definitively prove otherwise. In the Nelson case, first responders failed to establish a secure perimeter.

Reports indicate that responding officers did not erect crime-scene tape to restrict access. More critically, civilian witnesses informed NBC4 WCMH-TV that individuals inside the apartment were permitted to clean the floors with bleach while Nelson’s body was still in situ.

The introduction of sodium hypochlorite (household bleach) to a primary crime scene represents severe spoliation (the intentional, reckless, or negligent withholding, hiding, altering, or destroying of evidence). Bleach rapidly degrades DNA by breaking down the nucleic acids, rendering standard polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplification techniques ineffective. Any latent blood, saliva, or trace biological material present on the apartment floors was likely destroyed before forensic technicians could properly document, photograph, and collect it.

Autopsy Findings Contradict Initial Theories

The physical condition of Nelson’s remains at the scene contradicted the preliminary overdose theory. Nelson’s mother, Pam Nelson, and her sister, Abbie Vann, arrived at the apartment and immediately observed visible bruising and blood on the victim’s face.

The preliminary visual assessment by family members was later corroborated by forensic pathology. Upon the completion of the autopsy, the coroner officially determined Nelson’s cause of death (the specific injury or disease that leads to death) to be blunt force trauma. Consequently, the manner of death (the circumstances that result in death, categorized as natural, accident, suicide, homicide, or undetermined) was officially certified as a homicide.

Blunt force trauma indicates physical injury to a body part resulting from impact, injury, or physical attack with a dull object or surface, further rendering the initial local law enforcement assessment of an overdose forensically unsound.

Circumstantial Evidence and Community Interference

Faced with a compromised physical evidence record, investigators and the victim’s family have had to rely heavily on circumstantial evidence and community intelligence. In an effort to generate viable leads, Nelson’s family raised a $10,000 reward for information leading to an arrest and conviction.

Shortly after distributing reward posters throughout the tight-knit New Straitsville community, an unidentified individual was captured on local security cameras systematically removing nearly half of the flyers. While this action does not definitively indicate guilt in the homicide, behavioral analysis often correlates the destruction of investigative public appeals with individuals possessing localized knowledge of the crime or a direct desire to suppress community cooperation.

Prosecutorial Review and Current Investigative Status

Due to the initial chain-of-custody and scene preservation failures by the New Straitsville Police Department, the evidentiary burden required to secure an indictment is significantly higher. In December 2019, the investigative file, supplemented by efforts from the Ohio BCI, was submitted to Perry County Prosecutor Joe Flautt for review.

To date, no formal charges have been filed. The transition of the case file to the prosecutor’s office indicates that law enforcement has identified persons of interest or assembled a circumstantial narrative, but the lack of unassailable forensic data—likely lost during the initial scene contamination—remains a significant hurdle for trial viability.

The Evidence

  • Victim: Amy Jo Nelson, 39, discovered deceased on September 18, 2017, in a New Straitsville, Ohio, apartment.
  • Cause of Death: Blunt force trauma, determined by post-mortem autopsy.
  • Manner of Death: Homicide.
  • Scene Contamination: First responders failed to secure the perimeter. Civilians were permitted to use bleach on the floors near the body, effectively destroying potential DNA and trace biological evidence.
  • Initial Misclassification: The New Straitsville Police Department originally labeled the incident a suspected overdose, despite visible blood and bruising on the victim’s face.
  • Post-Incident Behavior: Security cameras captured an unidentified individual tearing down $10,000 reward posters distributed by the victim’s family.
  • Case Status: The investigative file was turned over to the Perry County Prosecutor in December 2019; no arrests have been made.

The Perry County Sheriff’s Office and the Ohio Bureau of Criminal Investigation continue to maintain jurisdiction over the Nelson homicide inquiry. Authorities and the family urge anyone with pertinent data to contact local law enforcement to help bridge the evidentiary gaps created in the critical first hours of the investigation.
