Genetic Genealogy Solves 44-Year California Cold Case

DNA extracted from fingernail scrapings and a discarded coffee cup enabled investigators to definitively link a former university employee to the 1974 Stanford murders.

Crime Docket March 25, 2026 771 words · 3 min read

The application of investigative genetic genealogy (IGG) to degraded biological samples has provided a definitive forensic resolution to a 44-year-old California homicide docket. By sequencing genetic material recovered from victim fingernail scrapings and cross-referencing public genealogy databases, law enforcement successfully secured the conviction of John Arthur Getreu for two murders committed in the 1970s.

This resolution highlights a distinct methodological pipeline that has become the standard in modern forensic investigations: extracting degraded DNA, generating profiles through specialized genomic laboratories, utilizing consumer databases to build reverse family trees, and finally, securing a covert confirmation sample for a legal indictment.

The Stanford Murders Forensic Pipeline

The technological foundation for this conviction began with evidence preserved from two distinct but geographically proximate crime scenes. In February 1973, 21-year-old Leslie Marie Perlov was found sexually assaulted and strangled with her own floral scarf in the Stanford University foothills. The following year, in March 1974, 21-year-old Janet Ann Taylor was found strangled in a roadside ditch in Woodside, California.

For decades, the physical evidence remained in evidence lockers without a match in CODIS (the FBI’s Combined DNA Index System). In 2018, the Santa Clara County Sheriff’s Office initiated a new forensic protocol. Detectives submitted male DNA found under Perlov’s fingernails to Virginia-based Parabon NanoLabs. Parabon utilized advanced sequencing to generate a comprehensive genetic profile, which was then uploaded to public consumer databases where users had opted into law enforcement matching.

Genealogists constructed family trees from distant cousins, tracing descendants forward to identify an individual matching the geographic and demographic profile of the unknown suspect. The genealogical data pointed directly to John Arthur Getreu, a 74-year-old former Stanford employee.

Surreptitious Collection and Indictment Data

While investigative genetic genealogy provides highly accurate investigative leads, courts require a direct, one-to-one forensic DNA match to issue an arrest warrant and secure an indictment. To satisfy this evidentiary burden, detectives initiated surveillance on Getreu in late 2018 and executed a surreptitious sampling protocol.

Investigators covertly collected a disposable coffee cup discarded by Getreu in Hayward, California. Laboratory technicians extracted DNA from the saliva left on the rim. This biological sample yielded a definitive, one-to-one match with the forensic evidence recovered from both Perlov’s fingernails and Taylor’s clothing.

The forensic match effectively closed the 44-year gap between the 1974 murder of Taylor and Getreu’s November 20, 2018 arrest.

Court records confirm the subsequent legal resolution: * San Mateo County Docket (Janet Taylor Case): Getreu was formally charged on May 16, 2019. Following a trial, a jury convicted him of first-degree murder on September 15, 2021. He was sentenced to life imprisonment. * Santa Clara County Docket (Leslie Perlov Case): On January 10, 2023, Getreu formally pleaded guilty to the 1973 murder of Perlov, ensuring his incarceration at the California Health Care Facility until his death in September 2023.

Expanding the Forensic Protocol

The methodology utilized in the Santa Clara and San Mateo county dockets mirrors the blueprint established by the apprehension of Joseph James DeAngelo, the Golden State Killer. In that watershed 2018 case, investigator Paul Holes and genealogist Barbara Rae-Venter utilized DNA recovered from a Ventura County crime scene, uploading the profile to GEDmatch and FamilyTreeDNA. By cross-referencing California driver’s license records for men with specific phenotypic traits within the identified family tree, the FBI narrowed the suspect pool, eventually confirming the match through a surreptitiously collected DNA sample.

Modern advancements in this pipeline now allow law enforcement to close cases even when the suspect is deceased. In the 1976 homicide of Karen Percifield, the Santa Cruz County Sheriff’s Office sent evidence to Othram, a Texas-based forensic genomics laboratory. Othram utilized Forensic-Grade Genome Sequencing® to build a comprehensive profile.

In April 2025, authorities announced that in-house genetic genealogy conclusively pointed to Richard Sommerhalder. Because Sommerhalder died in 1994, the district attorney could not file an indictment; however, the DNA match allowed detectives to definitively close the cold case file.

The Evidence

  • Victim DNA Extraction: The Santa Clara County Sheriff’s Office preserved and subsequently extracted male DNA from under the fingernails of 1973 homicide victim Leslie Marie Perlov.
  • Genetic Profiling: Parabon NanoLabs generated a viable genetic profile from the degraded sample, utilizing public databases to build a reverse family tree that identified John Arthur Getreu.
  • Covert Confirmation: Investigators collected a discarded disposable coffee cup from Getreu, yielding a one-to-one DNA match with biological evidence from two separate crime scenes.
  • Legal Resolution: The forensic match directly resulted in a first-degree murder conviction in San Mateo County (September 15, 2021) and a guilty plea in Santa Clara County (January 10, 2023), successfully closing the 44-year-old docket.
