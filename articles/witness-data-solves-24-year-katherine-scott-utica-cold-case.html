Witness Data Solves 24-Year Katherine Scott Utica Cold Case

The 2002 homicide of Katherine Scott has been closed by exception following the federal custody death of the primary suspect, which yielded critical new witness testimony.

Crime Docket March 24, 2026 974 words · 4 min read

A post-mortem determination of manual strangulation and the discovery of a body concealed in plastic beneath a mattress formed the initial evidentiary foundation in the 2002 Katherine Scott Utica cold case. Now, 24 years later, the Oneida County District Attorney’s Office and the Utica Police Department have formally closed the investigation. Authorities cite the death of the primary suspect and newly acquired witness testimony—which corroborates the original forensic theories—as the decisive factors in closing the file, according to statements released by local officials and reported by regional news outlet WKTV.

Crime Scene Pathology and Concealment

On January 10, 2002, responding officers from the Utica Police Department discovered the remains of 39-year-old Katherine Scott (also known in public records as Katherine Liggins) inside a residence at 16 Barnes Avenue in Utica, New York.

Crime scene technicians documented significant efforts to stage the scene and delay the discovery of the victim. Scott’s body was found wrapped in plastic and concealed beneath a mattress on the living room floor. In forensic pathology, the use of non-porous materials like plastic is often utilized by perpetrators in an attempt to contain physical decomposition and mask odors, thereby delaying law enforcement intervention and accelerating the degradation of trace evidence, such as transfer DNA or latent fingerprints.

Following crime scene processing, the victim was transported for an autopsy. The medical examiner ruled the manner of death a homicide. The specific cause of death was identified as manual strangulation and suffocation. Manual strangulation cases require sustained physical compression of the neck, typically resulting in distinct pathological markers such as petechial hemorrhaging (ruptured capillaries in the eyes and face) and trauma to the hyoid bone, which provided 2002 investigators with clear parameters regarding the proximity and physical capability of the offender.

Investigative Impediments and the Primary Suspect

Retired Utica Police Investigator Michael Acquaviva led the initial stages of the inquiry. Acquaviva’s early evidence collection successfully identified a primary suspect: Jarvis “Jabo” Simpson, a New York City native who was residing in Utica at the time of the murder.

Despite the physical evidence at the scene and investigative leads pointing toward Simpson, the case stalled. District attorneys operate under a strict burden of proof, requiring a high threshold of verifiable evidence to secure a grand jury indictment. The 2002 investigation lacked direct eyewitness testimony or a corroborated confession. Without these elements, or definitive DNA profiling directly linking Simpson to the act of strangulation (as opposed to mere presence in the residence), prosecutors could not legally pursue criminal charges.

Cold Case Strategy and Witness Psychology

As time progressed, the physical evidence was preserved while the investigative file transitioned through multiple hands, eventually reaching retired investigators Anthony Salerno and Tricia Nicholson-Scully. Nicholson-Scully, serving in a specialized role as a Senior Confidential Investigator for the Oneida County District Attorney’s Office, initiated a cold case strategy centered on behavioral analysis and repeated witness engagement.

According to regional reports from WIBX 950 and WKTV, Nicholson-Scully focused on persistently re-interviewing individuals tangentially connected to both Scott and Simpson. The objective of serial interviewing in cold cases is to identify inconsistencies in alibis over time or to capitalize on shifting loyalties among a suspect’s associates.

However, a primary psychological barrier remained: fear of retaliation. Witnesses in homicide cases involving suspects with extensive criminal histories often refuse to cooperate due to legitimate safety concerns. For over two decades, this fear kept the Katherine Scott investigation at a standstill.

Suspect’s Death Yields Verifiable Testimony

The trajectory of the investigation shifted definitively in 2024 due to an external legal development. Jarvis Simpson died while in federal custody on unrelated charges.

In forensic and cold case investigations, the death of a primary suspect frequently serves as a catalyst for breaking witness silence. Simpson’s death completely removed the threat of physical retaliation. Recognizing this shift in the investigative landscape, Senior Confidential Investigator Nicholson-Scully, alongside Assistant District Attorney Nicholas Fletcher, coordinated a renewed series of interviews.

This operational pivot proved successful. The Oneida County District Attorney’s Office confirmed that investigators successfully extracted substantial, verifiable information from associates who were previously uncooperative. These new testimonies provided the necessary corroborated links connecting Simpson directly to the 16 Barnes Avenue crime scene and the death of Katherine Scott.

The Evidence

  • Victim Identification: Katherine Scott (Katherine Liggins), 39, discovered deceased on January 10, 2002.
  • Crime Scene: 16 Barnes Avenue, Utica, New York. The body was deliberately concealed wrapped in plastic beneath a living room mattress.
  • Cause of Death: Ruled a homicide by the medical examiner, specifically resulting from manual strangulation and suffocation.
  • Suspect Identification: Jarvis “Jabo” Simpson was identified early in the 2002 investigation by Inv. Michael Acquaviva, but lack of witness corroboration prevented indictment.
  • Investigative Breakthrough: Simpson’s 2024 death in federal custody eliminated witness fear of retaliation, allowing Senior Confidential Investigator Tricia Nicholson-Scully to secure previously withheld, verifiable testimony.

Closed by Exception Classification

On January 8, 2026, Oneida County District Attorney Todd Carville and Utica Police Lt. Mike Curley officially announced the resolution of the Katherine Scott Utica cold case.

Because Jarvis Simpson is deceased, the prosecution cannot legally proceed with a grand jury indictment or a criminal trial. Under Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR) guidelines utilized by the FBI and local law enforcement, this case is designated as “closed by exception” or “exceptionally cleared.” This forensic and legal classification is applied when authorities have definitively identified the offender, possess sufficient evidence to support an arrest and formal charge, but encounter a circumstance outside the control of law enforcement that precludes prosecution—in this specific instance, the death of the perpetrator.

While no formal criminal charges will be filed, the Utica Police Department and the Oneida County District Attorney’s Office have officially closed the file, concluding a 24-year investigative effort driven by the preservation of forensic data and persistent witness pursuit.
