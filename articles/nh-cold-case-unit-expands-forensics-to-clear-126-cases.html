NH Cold Case Unit Expands Forensics to Clear 126 Cases

The New Hampshire Department of Justice deploys additional investigators to process an aging backlog of homicides and suspicious deaths using genetic genealogy.

Crime Docket March 24, 2026 842 words · 4 min read

Statistical analysis of the New Hampshire Department of Justice’s operational backlog reveals a concentrated inventory of 126 unresolved cases dating from the 1960s to the 2010s. To process this evidentiary threshold, the New Hampshire Cold Case Unit executed a strategic expansion in October 2025, deploying two additional full-time investigators to apply modern forensic methodologies to legacy case files. According to recent data published by the Concord Monitor, the unit is increasingly reliant on a combination of advanced DNA extraction, genetic genealogy, and localized public data sourcing to establish new investigatory leads.

Categorizing the NH Department of Justice Backlog

The 126 active files within the New Hampshire Cold Case Unit are divided into three distinct medico-legal categories, each presenting unique forensic hurdles. Based on October 2025 reporting by Rachel Wachman of the Concord Monitor, the inventory includes 97 unsolved homicides, 16 missing persons, and 13 suspicious deaths.

From a forensic standpoint, the distinction between these categories dictates the investigative approach. In the 97 unsolved homicides, a medical examiner has definitively ruled the manner of death as a homicide (death caused by the intentional action of another person), meaning law enforcement possesses a confirmed crime scene, a victim, and physical evidence, but lacks a verified suspect match.

Conversely, the 13 suspicious deaths represent cases where the circumstances of the fatality are irregular, but pathology reports and crime scene reconstruction lack the definitive physical evidence required to rule out accidental death, suicide, or natural causes. The 16 missing persons cases present the most significant physical challenge: the absence of a corpus delicti (the physical body of the crime), which restricts investigators from conducting autopsies or recovering trace evidence from remains.

Modern Forensics and Genetic Genealogy

Established in 2009, the NH Cold Case Unit has successfully cleared over a dozen legacy cases. Moving forward, the expanded task force aims to bridge the gap between aging evidence and contemporary technology.

Investigative leaders note that progress in these cases relies heavily on Forensic Genetic Genealogy (FGG). Legacy evidence—often consisting of degraded physiological material such as seminal fluid, blood, or hair follicles—was historically tested using STR (Short Tandem Repeat) analysis, which required a direct match in state or federal databases like CODIS (Combined DNA Index System). Today, forensic biologists can extract DNA from much smaller or environmentally degraded samples and utilize SNP (Single Nucleotide Polymorphism) profiling.

By generating an SNP profile from decades-old crime scene evidence, investigators can cross-reference the genetic data with open-source genealogical databases. This forensic technique allows analysts to map family trees and identify suspects through biological relatives, bypassing the need for the perpetrator’s DNA to be previously logged in a criminal database.

Evidentiary Hurdles in Legacy Homicides

The Concord Monitor investigation highlighted several specific victims, demonstrating the broad temporal and evidentiary scope of the state’s backlog.

The 1969 Homicide of Debra Horn: Forensic taphonomy (the study of how environmental conditions affect decomposing remains) plays a critical role in the unsolved homicide of 11-year-old Debra Horn. She disappeared from Allenstown, NH, in January 1969. Eight months later, her remains were located within the trunk of an abandoned vehicle in Sandown, approximately 25 miles away. The 56-year delay in justice is heavily influenced by the condition of the evidence; eight months of environmental exposure, temperature fluctuation, and insect activity inside a vehicle trunk severely degrades biological evidence, making standard DNA recovery highly difficult.

The 1978 Disappearance of Betty Place: Betty Place, a mother of six, was last seen at her Warner, NH, residence in June 1978. After 47 years, her body has never been recovered. Missing persons cases of this length rely heavily on secondary crime scenes or financial/digital footprint analysis. Without a body to process for cause of death or trace evidence transfer (Locard’s Exchange Principle, which states that every contact leaves a trace), investigators must rely heavily on civilian tips and circumstantial data.

The 2011 Homicide of John Labbe: John Labbe, 54, was discovered deceased from a gunshot wound inside a shed on his Plymouth, NH, property. Unlike the older cases, a 2011 scene benefits from modern initial processing. Forensic evidence in a firearm homicide typically includes ballistic material—specifically, microscopic striae (striations) on the recovered projectiles and ejector marks on the spent casings. These markers can be entered into NIBIN (National Integrated Ballistic Information Network) to link the weapon to other crimes, but solving the case ultimately requires a suspect match or witness corroboration.

The Evidence

  • Active Caseload: As of October 2025, the NH Cold Case Unit manages an active inventory of 126 unsolved cases.
  • Case Distribution: The backlog is categorized into 97 unsolved homicides, 16 missing persons, and 13 suspicious deaths.
  • Unit Expansion: The state added two new full-time investigators to the Cold Case Unit to process evidence using modern methodologies.
  • Forensic Application: The unit relies primarily on advanced DNA testing and genetic genealogy to re-examine legacy evidence, alongside localized public leads.
  • Historical Success: Since its inception in 2009, the NH Cold Case Unit has utilized forensic data to solve over a dozen long-standing cases.
